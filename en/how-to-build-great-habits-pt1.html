How to Build Great Habits (pt 1)

May 29, 2018

In my last post, I promised to write more about how to create great habits.

Why is knowing about habits helpful?

I started to look into habits, because I was struggeling to get myself to do things differently. I had tried the approach to “remind myself to do something”. That worked, but not very reliably: I would remind myself of the behaviour sometimes. But on a lot of days, I realized in the evening that I had forgotten what I wanted to do. And that was very frustrating. And not very effective. And energy consuming.

Then, after reading “The Power of Habit” by Charles Duhigg, I realized that there might be a better way: Habits.

Habits are basically what the brain uses to conserve (mental) energy. The “autopilot” part of the brain. And for me, this was an awesome value proposition: When I set up the right habit, my brain would remind myself of the desired behaviour all by itself. Awesome.

How do habits work?

Charles Duhigg explains that the “habit loop” has three major parts: a “cue” (or “trigger”), the behaviour itself, and a reward. Let’s have a quick look at the elements:

  • Cue/Trigger: This basically the start button for the habit. When our brain recognizes the trigger (after the habit is established), it will then start to execute the behaviour.

  • Behaviour: These are the thoughts and actions that the brain executes.

  • Reward: A positive emotion that signals “hey this was good” to the brain. It is the incentive for our brain to remember the trigger and the behaviour.

The Habit Loop is one of the core mechanism that our brain has used to learn for thousands of years. It requires no effort. In a nutshell, every time you feel a positive emotion, the brain asks itself: What was the environment like (cue/trigger) before or during the behaviour? What have I done (behaviour) before I felt the positive emotion?

Basically, the brain tries to figure out: When (cue) and I do (behaviour) then I get (reward). And after it has figured this out, the brain tries to improve the odds of getting the reward: it executes the behaviour when it recognizes the trigger.

The good news is: When you harness the power of this mechanism, you can help yourself to execute behaviours that are good for you more often.
