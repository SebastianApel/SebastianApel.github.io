How to mentally prepare when you want to become an Entrepreneur

Jun 29, 2018

Becoming an Entrepreneur is dangerous for your mental health

A significant number of people have the dream to become an Entrepreneur. Some of the dreamers embark on that journey. But: only very few make it. So far, so known.

Less known, but even more alarming is that a larger amout of Entrepreneurs seem to hurt themselves on the journey. Chris Myers has written on Forbes, that 30% of Entrepreuners are suffering from depression, while only 7% of the general population has that problem.

I’ve been asking myself: why is that the case?

Psychology to the rescue

When I look at this through the lens of Martin Seligmann’s theory of “Depression and Learned Helplessness”, the model states that a person will develop depression[2], when

  1. the person believes that the desired outcomes are improbable,
  2. or he/she believes that an undesired outcome is probable,
  3. and the person believes that she cannot do anything to change that

Welcome to Entrepreneurship. I can understand every Entrepreneur who get’s the feeling of “I can’t reach my desired outcome” after pouring heart and soul into a project that does not take off.

Carol Dweck, again

It get’s even more interesting when you look at Carol Dweck’s 1975 study “The role of expectations and attributions in the alleviation of learned helplessness”[3].

In a nutshell, she had participants work on problems. Some of the participants were allowed to “always be successful” by lowering the standard below their level of performance (SO=Success Only). Others were exposed to “failure” by setting the bar above their performance on purpose. In addition to communicating the failure, the researchers also offered an explanation to the participants: “You should have tried harder.” (AR=Attribution Retraining).

Then, during a test phase, the subjects were exposed to a “real” test scenario. In the “real” test scenario, both groups got the same procedure. The procedure contained “preplanned” failure scenario.

And (not really surprising): Those subjects in the AR group who had “practiced” failure where able to deal with the failure scenario and improve their performance. The group that was only used to be “successful” could not handle failure very well.

Interesting, but what does this have to do with learning Entrepreneurship?

Well, if your are used to be successful in whatever you have done before: Brace for impact. On the road to Entrepreneurship, stuff will not work as you want it to. And if you are not used to that, the fact that something is not working according to plan will have a detrimental effect on your performance. Not really a “virtuous circle”.

I find the following mindshift tremendously important:

  1. Try to build your self esteem around “showing up”, “trying” and “learning”. A good sentence for that might be “I am the kind of person that …”

  2. Check if curiosity helps: A sentence like “I wonder what happens if I try X” can do wonders

  3. “Practice” failure every day: Do hard stuff that you can not yet do - and train yourself to deal with the thoughts and emotions that come up then.
